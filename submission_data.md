# Conference Submission

## Title
**"Burn Your Code: Feature Files as the Ultimate AI Agent Prompt"**

---

## Abstract

We are at a pivotal moment: Software development is evolving from manual craftsmanship to AI orchestration. But as "Vibe Coding" becomes the new hype, we face a critical challenge: most teams still prompt an agent with "build me this", accept a plausible result, and move on without a durable specification or regression-safe control over the outcome. How do we move beyond fragile chat histories toward engineered precision? How do we ensure AI builds what the business needs without getting lost in translation or hallucinations?

The answer lies paradoxically in a technology often dismissed as "too slow": Behavior-Driven Development (BDD).

In this talk, I will demonstrate a workflow where Gherkin Feature Files become the primary "Single Source of Truth" for business behavior and a highly effective prompt for AI agents. We will explore the "Speed Paradox"—how the strict structure of BDD, which once slowed humans down, now provides a practical ramp for AI to deliver with more precision and reproducibility.

We will see how a classic Red/Green cycle serves as a guardrail, allowing us to treat implementations as easier to replace and compare. Just as importantly, we will look at the human review step that cannot be outsourced: checking step implementations for test honesty, because LLMs will optimize for "green" and may otherwise hide logic in the test harness. Discover how this setup bridges the gap between stakeholders and machines, reduces knowledge loss in teams, and makes legacy code easier to evolve through executable specifications.

---

## Short Abstract (Concise Version)

Software development is shifting from manual "Vibe Coding" to AI orchestration. The problem is that ad-hoc prompting rarely gives teams durable control or regression safety. This talk presents a workflow where BDD (Gherkin) serves as the primary source of truth for business behavior and as a reliable prompt for AI agents. By using the classic Red/Green cycle as a guardrail, plus explicit human review of step implementations for test honesty, we address the "Speed Paradox" and make implementations easier to compare, replace, and refactor.
